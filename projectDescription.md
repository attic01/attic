1. Core Definition
Attic is an online record store for independent artists, curated by Brian.

It is not conceived as an open, algorithm-driven music platform. Its value comes from personal taste, selective curation, and a direct relationship between independent musicians and listeners.

Core proposition
At Attic, users can:

Discover independent artists and releases

Purchase music directly

Keep purchased music inside a personal digital space called My Attic

Listen to purchased music through the app or web platform

Organize music into playlists

Share playlists with friends

Download purchased music as high-quality audio files

2. Product Philosophy
Attic should become an ecosystem where musicians and fans support one another.

The relationship should feel like:

A direct connection built through mutual co-arising.

The product should not feel like a large-scale streaming service. It should feel more like a personal record shop and listening room shaped by human taste.

Design principles
Intimate

Personal

Quiet

Minimal

3. Primary User Experience
3.1 Discover music
Users enter Attic and discover music through editorial curation rather than endless algorithmic feeds.

Discovery can happen through:

Newly joined artists

New releases

Trending playlists

Artist pages

Album pages

Curated record selections

Written editorial content

3.2 Purchase music
Users purchase individual songs or albums.

A purchase gives the user:

Permanent access inside My Attic

In-platform listening

A high-quality downloadable audio file

The ability to add the music to playlists

The ability to share playlists containing the music

The note suggests setting a minimum price for each song while allowing the buyer to voluntarily pay more.

This resembles a “pay at least this amount” model.

3.3 Own and listen
Purchased music is stored in My Attic, the user’s personal library.

My Attic functions as both:

A personal record collection

A music player

Users can revisit, organize, and listen to everything they have purchased.

The logo gives me a very specific combination of feelings:

Overall vibe
Warm, handmade, playful, nostalgic, and slightly strange.

It does not feel like a polished tech platform or a conventional record label. It feels more like:

a small independent record shop

a hand-painted sign above a basement venue

a record sleeve from the 1970s

a personal collection assembled by someone with distinct taste

something discovered, not advertised

The rounded lettering makes Attic feel soft and welcoming, while the brown-and-cream palette gives it an earthy, aged quality. It feels analog even though the product is digital.

What the logo communicates well
1. Human curation
The irregular letterforms look made by a person rather than generated by a system. That supports your idea that Attic is curated by a specific human perspective—not an anonymous marketplace or algorithm.

2. Intimacy
The logo feels small-scale in a good way. It suggests that Attic is a place you enter quietly, where someone has chosen what belongs there.

It says:

“Come look through what I’ve found.”

rather than:

“Browse millions of tracks.”

3. Music history without being retro cosplay
It has a vintage record-store feeling, but it does not look locked into one genre. It could work for jazz, hip-hop, indie rock, soul, experimental music, or Korean underground releases.

4. Playfulness without looking childish
The large “Attic” lettering is almost cartoon-like, but the restrained palette and thin “records” lettering keep it from becoming overly cute. It feels eccentric rather than juvenile.

Emotional associations
The strongest words that come to mind are:

Dusty, warm, tactile, curious, private, offbeat, soulful, collectible, imperfect, trusted.

It feels like late afternoon light, wooden furniture, old sleeves, handwritten recommendations, and music playing from another room.

The brand personality it suggests
Attic feels like a person who:

knows a lot about music but is not pretentious

enjoys obscure things but does not gatekeep

values emotional connection over hype

keeps physical objects and remembers where they came from

introduces you to music personally

prefers character over perfection

That is a strong foundation for the project because the logo already supports the core promise of Attic: music selected and passed from one person to another.

What kind of product design matches it
The interface should not become too sleek, futuristic, or platform-like. A cold black-and-white streaming interface would fight against this identity.

The logo suggests a visual world built from:

warm browns, cream, faded yellow, muted red, dark charcoal

generous empty space

slightly irregular typography

paper, wood, fabric, dust, print, and sleeve textures

editorial photography rather than commercial promotional imagery

handwritten annotations used sparingly

simple layouts that resemble record sleeves, catalogues, or shop displays

gentle motion rather than fast transitions

The key is to preserve the handmade feeling without making the product difficult to use. The structure can be extremely clean, while the surfaces and details feel personal.

One tension to be aware of
The logo leans charming and playful. Your concept, however, also includes ownership, artist support, high-quality audio, and serious curation.

So the rest of the identity should add some quiet authority around it. The logo can remain warm and loose, but the supporting typography, photography, spacing, and editorial voice should feel considered and confident.

I would describe the ideal balance as:

A playful sign outside, a serious record collection inside.

The clearest brand direction I feel from it is:

Attic is a warm, human-curated digital record shop for music worth keeping.

Attic MVP Implementation Plan
Scope: Frontend prototype only, with no backend or real payment system

The MVP should prove one central experience:

A listener discovers a carefully presented independent release, purchases it through a simulated flow, and finds it inside a personal music collection called My Attic.

The goal is not to build a complete music platform yet. It is to demonstrate the product’s identity, navigation, ownership model, and core listening experience clearly enough for internal review, artist conversations, and early user testing.

1. MVP Product Goals
The first version should answer five questions:

Does Attic feel meaningfully different from a streaming platform?

Can users understand that music is purchased and kept?

Does human curation make discovery feel valuable?

Does My Attic feel like a personal collection?

Can the visual identity feel warm and handmade without hurting usability?

The MVP should prioritize emotional clarity and complete user journeys over feature breadth.

2. Core MVP User Journey
The main demo flow should be:

User enters the Attic landing page.

User browses curated releases.

User opens an album page.

User previews selected tracks.

User starts a simulated purchase.

User completes the purchase through a mock checkout.

The album appears in My Attic.

User plays the purchased album.

User adds tracks to a playlist.

User views or shares the playlist through a simulated share action.

This single journey should feel complete before additional features are added.
